# Climate Change: Science, Impact, and Solutions

## Understanding Climate Change

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While climate variations occur naturally — through volcanic eruptions, solar cycles, and orbital changes — the current rapid warming is primarily driven by human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels since the Industrial Revolution began in the 1750s.

## The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms the Earth's surface. When the Sun's energy reaches Earth's atmosphere, some is reflected back to space, while the rest is absorbed by the land and oceans, heating the surface. The Earth radiates heat back toward space as infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere — including carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases — absorb and re-emit this infrared radiation, trapping heat and warming the planet.

Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be about -18°C (0°F) instead of the current 15°C (59°F). However, human activities have significantly increased greenhouse gas concentrations, enhancing this effect and causing additional warming.

## Key Evidence

### Temperature Records
Global average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C since pre-industrial times. The last decade (2014-2023) was the warmest on record. Each of the last four decades has been successively warmer than any preceding decade since 1850.

### Ocean Changes
Oceans absorb about 90% of excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases. This has led to:
- Sea surface temperatures rising by 0.88°C since 1900
- Ocean acidification (pH decreased by 0.1 units since pre-industrial times)
- Deoxygenation in many marine regions
- Coral bleaching events becoming more frequent and severe

### Ice and Snow
- Arctic sea ice has declined by about 13% per decade since 1979
- The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are losing mass at accelerating rates
- Mountain glaciers worldwide are retreating
- Snow cover in the Northern Hemisphere has decreased, particularly in spring

### Sea Level Rise
Global mean sea level has risen approximately 20 cm since 1900, with the rate accelerating to 3.7 mm per year in the last decade. Projections suggest 0.3 to 1.0 meters of additional rise by 2100, depending on emission scenarios.

## Impacts

### Extreme Weather Events
Climate change increases the frequency and intensity of many extreme weather events:
- **Heatwaves**: More frequent, longer, and more intense
- **Heavy Precipitation**: Increased rainfall intensity leading to floods
- **Droughts**: More severe and prolonged in some regions
- **Tropical Cyclones**: Greater intensity and rainfall, though frequency may not increase
- **Wildfires**: Extended fire seasons and larger burned areas

### Ecosystems and Biodiversity
- Species are shifting their ranges toward poles and higher elevations
- Timing of seasonal events (migration, flowering) is changing
- Coral reefs face severe threats from warming and acidification
- Up to one million species face extinction risk from climate change and related factors

### Human Systems
- **Agriculture**: Changed growing seasons, crop yield impacts, increased pest pressure
- **Health**: Heat-related mortality, expanded disease ranges, air quality degradation
- **Water Resources**: Altered precipitation patterns affecting water availability
- **Infrastructure**: Damage from extreme events, coastal flooding risks
- **Migration**: Climate-related displacement affecting millions annually

## Solutions

### Mitigation (Reducing Emissions)
- **Renewable Energy**: Solar, wind, and hydroelectric power replacing fossil fuels
- **Energy Efficiency**: Better buildings, transportation, and industrial processes
- **Electrification**: Electric vehicles, heat pumps, industrial electrification
- **Carbon Capture**: Technologies to remove CO2 from atmosphere or point sources
- **Nature-Based Solutions**: Reforestation, wetland restoration, sustainable agriculture

### Adaptation (Adjusting to Changes)
- **Infrastructure**: Climate-resilient buildings, flood defenses, cooling systems
- **Agriculture**: Drought-resistant crops, improved irrigation, diversified farming
- **Early Warning Systems**: Better forecasting and disaster preparedness
- **Ecosystem Management**: Protected areas, wildlife corridors, assisted migration
- **Urban Planning**: Green infrastructure, heat island mitigation, managed retreat from coasts

## International Agreements

The Paris Agreement (2015) committed nations to limiting global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, pursuing efforts for 1.5°C. Countries submit Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) outlining their emission reduction plans. As of 2024, current policies put the world on track for approximately 2.5-2.9°C of warming by 2100, highlighting the need for increased ambition.
